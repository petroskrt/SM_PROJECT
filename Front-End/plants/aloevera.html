Aloe Vera

Your Guide to growing and caring for Aloe Vera

About Aloe Vera

Aloe Vera, is a succulent plant species of the genus Aloe.[4] It is widely distributed, and is considered an invasive species in many world regions. An evergreen perennial, it originates from the Arabian Peninsula, but also grows wild in tropical, semi-tropical, and arid climates around the world. It is cultivated for commercial products, mainly as a topical treatment used over centuries. The species is considered attractive for decorative purposes, and is often used indoors as a potted plant.

Care Requirements

Light

Thrives in bright, indirect sunlight. Can tolerate some direct sun, but too much intense afternoon sun may cause leaves to turn brown. Ideal location is near a south or west-facing window.

Water

Water deeply but infrequently - allow soil to dry completely between waterings (usually every 2-3 weeks). Water even less during winter. Signs of overwatering include soft, mushy leaves and root rot.

Humidity & Temperature

Prefers temperatures between 55-80°F (13-27°C). Can survive brief periods of cold but not frost. Tolerates low humidity, making it perfect for indoor environments.

Soil & Drainage

Well-draining cactus or succulent mix is essential. Add perlite or coarse sand to improve drainage if needed. Always use a pot with drainage holes.

Benefits

  • Medicinal properties - gel can be used for minor burns, sunburns, and skin irritations
  • Air purifying capabilities - helps remove formaldehyde and benzene from the air
  • Drought-resistant and low maintenance
  • Can be propagated easily through pups (offsets)
  • Long-lasting and can live for many years with proper care
  • Attractive architectural plant that works well in modern décor

Common Problems

  • Brown leaves - Usually indicates too much direct sunlight or sunburn
  • Soft, mushy leaves - Typically a sign of overwatering
  • Thin, curling leaves - Shows signs of underwatering
  • Leggy growth - Insufficient light causing the plant to stretch
  • Yellow, transparent leaves - Often due to overwatering or poor drainage
  • Root rot - Caused by excessive moisture and poor drainage

Harvesting & Use Tips

When harvesting aloe gel:

  • Choose mature leaves from the outer portion of the plant
  • Cut leaves at the base, as close to the stem as possible
  • Allow the yellow sap (aloin) to drain before collecting the gel
  • Only harvest what you need - the gel doesn't store well long-term
  • Never harvest more than 1/3 of the plant's leaves at once

Notes

While aloe vera gel is generally safe for topical use, the yellow sap (latex) found just under the skin can cause skin irritation in some people and should not be ingested. Keep away from pets as it can be toxic if eaten.
